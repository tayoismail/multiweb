BMI Calculator — Calculate Your Body Mass Index

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple, widely-used measure that estimates whether a person has a healthy body weight relative to their height. It is calculated by dividing a person's weight in kilograms by the square of their height in meters. Healthcare professionals use BMI as a screening tool to categorize individuals into weight categories — underweight, normal weight, overweight, or obese — which can help identify potential health risks associated with body weight.


While BMI is not a direct measure of body fat or overall health, it correlates reasonably well with body fatness for most adults. Studies have shown that BMI is a useful indicator for identifying population-level weight trends and screening for conditions like cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and hypertension. However, it has important limitations — it doesn't account for muscle mass, bone density, body composition, age, gender, or ethnic differences.

The BMI Formula

BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height (m)²
Example: A person who weighs 70 kg and is 1.75 m tall: BMI = 70 ÷ (1.75 × 1.75) = 70 ÷ 3.06 = 22.9 (Normal weight)

Imperial: BMI = (weight in lbs × 703) ÷ height (in)²
Example: A person who weighs 154 lbs and is 69 inches tall: BMI = (154 × 703) ÷ (69 × 69) = 108,262 ÷ 4,761 = 22.7 (Normal weight)

Health Implications of BMI Categories

Underweight (< 18.5): Being underweight may indicate malnutrition, digestive disorders, or other underlying health conditions. It can lead to weakened immune function, osteoporosis, and fertility issues. If your BMI falls in this range, consider consulting a healthcare provider to identify the cause and develop a plan to achieve a healthy weight.

Normal (18.5 – 24.9): A BMI in this range is associated with the lowest risk of weight-related health problems. However, it's important to maintain a balanced diet and regular physical activity to support overall health, regardless of BMI. This range is considered optimal for most adults.

Overweight (25 – 29.9): Excess weight increases the risk of developing chronic conditions such as heart disease, type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, and certain cancers. Even modest weight loss (5-10% of body weight) can significantly reduce these risks and improve metabolic health.

Obese (30+): Obesity is a serious health condition that substantially increases the risk of cardiovascular disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, sleep apnea, joint problems, and several types of cancer. If your BMI is 30 or higher, it's strongly recommended to consult a healthcare professional for a comprehensive assessment and personalized weight management plan.

Limitations of BMI

While BMI is a useful screening tool, it has several important limitations that should be considered when interpreting your results:

  • Muscle mass: Athletes and people with high muscle mass may have a high BMI despite having very low body fat. Muscle weighs more than fat, so BMI can overestimate body fat in muscular individuals.
  • Age: Older adults typically have more body fat than younger adults at the same BMI. BMI does not account for age-related changes in body composition.
  • Gender: Women generally have a higher percentage of body fat than men at the same BMI. The same BMI cutoffs are used for both genders, which may not accurately reflect health risks for everyone.
  • Ethnicity: People of Asian descent may have higher health risks at a lower BMI compared to other populations. Some studies suggest that the healthy BMI range for Asian populations should be lower (18.5 – 23).
  • Body composition: BMI does not distinguish between fat mass and lean mass, nor does it indicate where fat is distributed in the body. Abdominal fat (belly fat) is more strongly linked to health risks than fat stored in other areas.
